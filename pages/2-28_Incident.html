2-28 Incident

The 2-28 Incident (Chinese: 二二八事件; Pinyin: Èrèrbā shìjiàn), also known as the February 28 Massacre (Chinese: 二二八大屠殺; Èrèrbā dà túshā) refers to the violent islandwide unrest beginning on February 28, 1947, and their brutal aftermath.

The events were sparked by the arrest of a woman illegally selling cigarettes–which were a government monopoly at the time–in Taipei on February 27, 1979. The woman resisted, and small crowd gathered to confront the police officers, which led to one of the policemen firing his gun into the crowd, killing a bystander. The next day, people gathered in front of government buildings, such as the Monopoly Bureau Headquarters to protest the events of the previous day, but the events grew more violent as time moved on. Two Monopoly Commission agents were killed and ROC authorities were eventually rendered unable to function. The admisistration temporarily declared martial law on February 28.

The newly de facto leaders of Taiwan–mainly the Taiwanese elite–promptly began negotiating with Governor Chen Yi to resolve the crisis. On March 7, they confronted the administration with 32 demands, which included ending military rule, more inclusive governance and granting greater autonomy to Taiwan as a province. Chen had asked for and received reionforcments from the Mainland, who landed on March 8-10 at Keelung and Kaohsiung and restoring order by force by March 13.

It is difficult to precisely estimate the number of casualties, as the subsequent crackdowns were mainly carried out in secret. Estimates range from the extremes of a few hundred to a few hundred thousand, but the KMT's records suggest the casualties to have been above 28,000.1
