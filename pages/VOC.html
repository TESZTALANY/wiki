Dutch East India Company

The Dutch East India Company, or VOC, was a Dutch charter company established in 1602 with the purpose of facilitating trade with and colonising South and East Asia.

They first established a settlement on Taiwan near present-day Tainan in 1623. Bar the 16 year period from the 1626 establishment of the Spanish Fort San Salvador at Keelung to its conquering by the joint forces of the VOC and indigenous tribes, they remained the sole hegemons of the island until the 1661 siege of Fort Zeelandia by the fleet of Zheng Chenggong

Besides operating harbors, they traded for deer meat and hyde with the Austronesians and for sugarcane and rice farmed by settlers from China. They utilized divide and conquer strategies to incite ethnic conflict between the Hokkien-speaking Chinese settlers from the Zhangzhou and Quanzhou regions, Hakka-speaking Chinese, many of whom were deported from southern China and the natives.

Under their rule, settlements populated by Chinese farmers and fishermen became increasingly common. While they maintained ownership of the land, they allowed the farmers considerable autonomy.

They were eventually defeated in 1661 by the fleet of Zheng Chenggong, which forced them to abandon the island. They reesatblished a foothold in the north at Keelung in 1664 but lost it in 1668.
