Qing Dynasty

The Qing Dynasty was imperial China's last dynasty, coming to power in the mid-1600s and formally dissolving after the abdication of its last emperor in 1912.

The Qing seized taiwan in 1683 and incorporated it into their empire as part of Fujian province. Their reign over Taiwan was characterised by weakness and incompleteness, in part due to phisical limitations caused by the island's geography, much of the island remaining under the control of Ming-loyalist triads and secret societies, and to Taiwan being viewed as the outer edge of the Chisese Empire, its only purpose being to not be a warmbed of unrests that would drain the empire's resources.

The light interference meant immense economic freedoms that lead to Taiwan becoming a major exporter of goods, such as fish, timber, tea, indigo dye, but especially rice and sugar, by the mid-18th century.

As the Late Qing Dynasty began to fall in the latter half of the 19th century due to external threats and domestic rebellions, the British took over tax and tariff collection on the island. The island began its modernisation in the 1870s under Governor Liu and in 1887, Taiwan became an independent province, though following decisive Japanese victory in the First Sino-Japanese War, China ceded the island of Taiwan to Japan, who /// continued it ///
